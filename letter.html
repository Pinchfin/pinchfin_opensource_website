A Note from the Builder

I built pinchfin while running a lending platform.

Not in a lab. Not in a venture-backed sprint. Between calls with capital partners, between closings on transitional multifamily loans, between the daily work of originating, underwriting, and managing a portfolio of commercial real estate debt.

Twenty-five years of structuring transactions across institutional balance sheets taught me something most technology companies building for this space have not lived: the methodology is everything. The constraint hierarchies, the underwriting assumptions, the way a five-constraint waterfall identifies the binding limitation on a $75 million bridge loan — that is what makes analysis defensible at the committee table. Not the interface. Not the model. The methodology.

pinchfin is that methodology made executable. 192,000+ lines of structured analytical code. 24 modules. 229 skills. 81 governance nodes. A 17-phase orchestration framework that separates deterministic financial computation from inference — so every debt service coverage ratio, every loan sizing output, every cash flow projection is reproducible, auditable, and independently verifiable.

I considered releasing this as open source. I spent real time on that question.

The honest answer: the system is too sophisticated to hand someone a repository and say “configure it yourself.” The credit box alone encodes hundreds of institutional parameters. The workflow spans 17 sequential phases with four blocking gates. The three-agent QA architecture requires specific orchestration to maintain analytical integrity. Source code without execution infrastructure is a cockpit without an airplane.

So I landed on the MCP.

The Model Context Protocol gives users the full power of the platform — every module, every skill, every governance node, every phase of the pipeline — without requiring them to implement, configure, or maintain the underlying system. You bring your analytical environment. pinchfin brings 25 years of institutional credit methodology, codified and executable.

The marginal cost is insignificant relative to the output. A constraint waterfall sizing that takes a senior analyst hours executes in minutes with full audit trail. The investment committee memorandum alone — a capstone that synthesizes sponsor analysis, market data, comparables, cash flow underwriting, and sizing rationale into a committee-ready document — justifies the platform on a single transaction.

And here is what I believe is the most powerful capability: the credit box is yours.

Every lender operates with a different credit philosophy. Your DSCR floors, your LTV ceilings, your debt yield minimums, your geographic preferences, your property type restrictions — these define your institutional identity. Out of the box, pinchfin ships with market-standard credit parameters calibrated to institutional multifamily bridge lending. But with full membership, the platform does two things that I have not seen anywhere else in this market:

First, it helps you build your credit soul. Through our proprietary deal analysis and synthesis module, pinchfin processes your transaction history, your credit decisions, your committee feedback — and identifies the patterns that define how your firm actually makes lending decisions. Not how your policy manual says you make them. How you actually make them.

Second, it implements your custom credit box. Your constraint hierarchies. Your underwriting assumptions. Your policy overlays. Configured and maintained so that every analysis the platform produces reflects your firm's credit heartbeat — not a generic industry template.

Whether you are a brokerage analyst running bridge loan comps or an originations team sizing a portfolio acquisition, the platform adapts to your institutional standards while maintaining the analytical rigor of the underlying framework.

The underwriting workflow works. It produces institutional-quality outputs that I would put in front of any credit committee I have ever sat on — and I have sat on several backed by some of the largest institutional allocators in commercial real estate.

This is the product of building something real while doing the real work.
